Making the Elite: Caste, Class, and Cultural Capital in Multinational Hiring

Short Summary: Twitter thread
Coverage: Steven Pinker (x2); Raghuram Rajan; Alice Evans; Marginal Revolution
Class disparities persist in elite hiring, but where do they arise and why? India's codified caste categories provide a tractable proxy for cumulative social class, allowing me to identify where disparities emerge and to evaluate whether cultural-capital screening is a central channel through which caste-linked disadvantage operates via class-coded biographical cues. Using screening data from over 1,000 jobs at multinationals recruiting at an elite Indian college, I follow candidates across four rounds and show that subjective personal interviews assessing "cultural fit" account for over 95% of the caste success-rate penalty. Two tests triangulate the mechanism. First, large language model analysis of nearly one million words from publicly available mock-preparation transcripts shows that personal interviews emphasize biographical markers of cultural capital—hometown, schooling, hobbies, family background, and parents' occupation—while earlier subjective rounds such as group debates focus on abstract policy reasoning. Second, surname timing shows penalties at application screening for the 15% of students with caste-identifiable surnames, but only after fit interviews for the 85% with initially ambiguous surnames, consistent with a pattern in which, for the majority, disparities emerge when interview content makes cultural-capital signals legible to evaluators rather than from in-person cues alone. Disadvantaged-caste hires show lower attrition and higher promotion rates across job selectivity tiers and role types, patterns difficult to reconcile with simple customer or accurate statistical discrimination. Interviews with senior HR heads at five multinationals point to organizational frictions—screener-supervisor separation and weak performance feedback—that may sustain biased fit screening.

Interviews with Jason Sockin and Elliott Ash

Short Summary: Twitter thread
Links: CEPR Working Paper
Interviews allow employers to learn about workers, but do they also enable workers to learn about firms? Studying 500,000 interview reports from Glassdoor, we find candidates for high-paying jobs are more likely to reject a job offer if they believe the interview was easy. Easy interviews appear to convey poor "fit" as those who accept offers after easy interviews are two-fifths of a standard deviation less satisfied with their jobs and 10 percent less likely to remain with their employer for at least one year. Analysis of interview narratives using large language models reveals difficult interviews signal colleague ability whereas easy interviews convey a nonselective process. In a small-scale randomized field experiment, an exogenous increase in difficulty elevated perceived difficulty and boosted applicant engagement with the vacancy. Interviews offer workers a preview of match quality, highlighting a channel through which labor markets may become less efficient if firms automate hiring with AI.
