Research Papers

  • Making the Elite: Class Discrimination at Multinationals

    • Short Summary: Twitter thread
    Coverage: Steven Pinker; Breaking the Mold: India's Untraveled Path to Prosperity (Raghuram Rajan); The American Saga; Marginal Revolution


    How salient is hiring discrimination in driving socioeconomic gaps in access to elite jobs, and where in the hiring process do these disparities arise? These questions are particularly challenging to answer regarding social status or class discrimination in elite hiring. Although such discrimination is widely acknowledged, identifying its source has proven elusive in Western contexts, where social position must be inferred from multiple markers such as accent, education, and networks. India’s caste system provides a uniquely clear measure of social status, allowing more precise identification of where and how status shapes access to top jobs. Analyzing detailed screening data from US and European multinationals hiring in India, this paper provides the first systematic evidence quantifying both the salience and source of social status or class discrimination in elite hiring. Subjective personal interviews assessing “fit” are the primary mechanism for revealing and penalizing social position in elite job recruitment. Nearly 90% of caste disparities in hiring emerge during such fit assessments. The higher promotion rates of disadvantaged castes—about 20% even in the most selective jobs—suggest that any information gained during personal interviews cannot justify these hiring disparities. These findings reveal how subjective “fit”-focused assessments in elite hiring can systematically exclude high-potential talent based on social status. This presents a fundamental challenge for organizations worldwide: how can they retain necessary managerial discretion in hiring while preventing the kind of status-based discrimination that undermines both diversity and talent optimization?
